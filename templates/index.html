Most risk tools stop at likelihood. This one treats harm as a first-class variable.

A threat that's likely but harmless and a threat that's rare but catastrophic are not the same problem — yet many risk-scoring approaches collapse them into one number. Here, harm gets its own distribution, its own uncertainty, and its own weight in the final score, separate from how often something is expected to happen.

That distinction is what lets the model tell the difference between "frequent but minor" and "rare but severe," and rank control sets accordingly — not just by how many threats they touch, but by how much those threats actually cost if they land.

Pipeline

Four steps, independent of domain

01 — Threats

Describe what can go wrong

Each threat gets a likelihood and a harm distribution. Harm doesn't have to mean "patient injury" — it's whatever downside your system cares about, scaled to [0,1].

02 — Controls

Describe what you can do about it

Each control gets a cost range and an effectiveness distribution — the fraction of risk it removes from any threat it's mapped to.

03 — Map & sample

Connect controls to threats, then simulate

Every possible subset of controls is tested. For each, harm and effectiveness are sampled from their distributions — not just averaged.

04 — Rank

Score and sort

Subsets that breach the threshold on any single threat are discarded. The rest are ranked by the Risk Score Index — lowest is best.

Formal definitions

The math

Three quantities in this model carry real uncertainty — implementation cost, control effectiveness, and patient/system harm — and each is modeled with a distribution chosen for a specific reason, not by default.

Residual risk, per threat
Ri = Li · Hi · ∏j∈S(1 − Ej)

L is likelihood, H is sampled harm, S is the set of controls active against threat i, and Ej is the sampled effectiveness of control j. Multiple controls compound multiplicatively — each removes a fraction of what's left.

Risk Score Index
RSI = Σ λk · nk

Each threat's residual risk is bucketed into five levels relative to the threshold (low → high), each weighted (0.1 → 0.9), then summed. One number per control set, ranked ascending.

Implementation cost Log-Normal(μ, σ)

Cost is typically the product of several independent factors — labor, materials, training, integration — each scaling the total multiplicatively rather than adding to it. That kind of multiplicative combination of factors is exactly what produces a log-normal shape in practice: strictly positive, clustered around a moderate value, with a long right tail that allows for occasional large overruns. σ is calibrated so the estimated maximum cost sits near the 95th percentile, and μ follows from the minimum estimate.

Control effectiveness Beta(α, β)

There's no universally "correct" distribution for how effective a security or safety control turns out to be — but the value is always bounded between 0 (no effect) and 1 (fully effective), and the right shape needs to flex with how many factors could degrade that effectiveness. A control whose performance depends on one clear factor should have a narrow spread; one exposed to many independent failure modes needs a wider one. Beta handles both ends of that range — it can be tight and roughly symmetric, or skewed, or U-shaped — by adjusting just two parameters, which is why each control here can carry its own, independently calibrated, level of uncertainty.

Harm to the affected party Beta(α, β)

The impact of a realized threat — scored from 0 (no impact) to 1 (worst-case outcome) — is an open problem with no single agreed-upon distribution either. Beta was chosen for the same flexibility reason as effectiveness, with the added support of prior work: researchers modeling continuous, bounded health-impact outcomes have used Beta successfully to represent combinations of patient risk, and it has also been applied to quality-of-life impact scores constrained to the same [0,1] range. A Poisson model was considered and set aside — it's built for count data, and is too rigid to capture the shape of a continuous, bounded harm outcome the way Beta can.

Where the numbers come from

Estimates you can defend, not numbers you have to trust blindly

Likelihood, harm and effectiveness are judgment calls dressed up as numbers. The model doesn't pretend otherwise — it gives you a structured place to record why you chose a value, so the estimate is auditable even when it isn't exact.

For an existing system

Use incident history, penetration-test findings, or vendor security assessments. These are your most defensible numbers because they're observed, not estimated.

For a new design

Use published vulnerability research for comparable systems, industry breach reports, or vendor-published control effectiveness (e.g. NIST, OWASP). Note the source in the optional justification field.

When you genuinely don't know

Use a wide variance rather than guessing a narrow one — the model is built to carry that uncertainty through to the result rather than hide it in a false-precision point estimate.

Sanity-check the output, not just the input

If the optimal set surprises you, that's often the model finding a cheap control with outsized reach across many threats — check the mapping before you check the math.
