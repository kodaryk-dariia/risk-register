Threats

Likelihood How often you'd expect this threat to actually happen, from 0 (basically never) to 1 (essentially certain). A guessable starting point: rare ≈ 0.1–0.3, plausible ≈ 0.4–0.6, expected ≈ 0.7–0.9.
Harm Mean Your best single guess at how bad it is if this threat succeeds, from 0 (no real impact) to 1 (worst case). This is separate from likelihood — a rare threat can still have a high harm value.
Harm Variance How unsure you are about that harm guess. Small (e.g. 0.005) means "I'm fairly confident." Larger (e.g. 0.03) means "it could really vary depending on circumstances." When in doubt, use a larger number rather than a falsely confident small one.
Example: "An attacker intercepts unencrypted data" — happens fairly often if nothing's encrypted (Likelihood 0.7), and if it happens the data exposed is sensitive but not life-threatening (Harm Mean 0.4), and you're reasonably confident in that estimate (Harm Variance 0.01).

Security Controls

Cost Min / Max Your realistic low and high estimate for what this control costs to implement, in dollars — labor, tools, licenses, setup. Doesn't need to be exact; a sensible range is enough.
Effectiveness Mean How much of the risk this control removes when it's in place, from 0 (does nothing) to 1 (eliminates the threat entirely). Most real controls land around 0.7–0.95 — very few things are perfect.
Effectiveness Variance How much that effectiveness might swing depending on setup, configuration, or attacker skill. Small (e.g. 0.004) for a well-understood control; larger (e.g. 0.02) if effectiveness really depends on how well it's deployed.
Example: "TLS 1.3 encryption" costs somewhere between $300–$7,000 to roll out properly (Cost Min/Max), is usually very effective when configured correctly (Effectiveness Mean 0.95), and effectiveness rarely varies much once it's set up right (Effectiveness Variance 0.002).
